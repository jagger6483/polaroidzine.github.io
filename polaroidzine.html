Not for Radio: María Zardoya’s Melt and the Art of Reclaiming Quiet

By Polaroid Zine Staff — September 2025

María Zardoya has always understood atmosphere. As the hypnotic voice of The Marías, she helped define the dream-pop sound of the late 2010's,  silky vocals, hazy synths, and a kind of cinematic melancholy. But her new project, Not for Radio, feels like something else entirely. With her debut solo album Melt, released this fall, Zardoya steps into solitude and lets silence become part of the music.

A Different Frequency

The name Not for Radio says it all. This isn’t an album chasing hits or streaming algorithm,  it’s one that drifts, breathes, and resists polish. In interviews, Zardoya described the record as “music made without trying to please anyone.” Recorded over three quiet weeks in upstate New York with longtime collaborator Luca Buccellati and producer Sam Evian, Melt is more personal than anything she’s released before.

The sound is stripped-down yet immersive: analog synths hum beneath reverb-coated guitars, and Zardoya’s voice — softer, more human than ever — floats in and out of focus. Each track feels like it’s happening in real time, caught between memory and invention.

Themes of Solitude and Self-Reconstruction

“Melt” isn’t about heartbreak in the traditional sens,  it’s about what happens after. The songs move through loneliness, longing, and a kind of fragile rebirth. On tracks like “Fever Dream”  and “Glass House,” Zardoya wrestles with detachment and the fear of becoming unrecognizable to yourself. The lyrics often read like journal entries: short, confessional, and almost whispered.

Where The Marías’ work often felt cinematic and curated, Melt feels unfiltered like a late-night voice memo you weren’t meant to hear. It’s the sound of a woman asking who she is when the cameras and collaborators are gone.

Why It Matters

With Not for Radio, Zardoya isn’t just starting a side project,  she’s redefining what creative freedom looks like in a world obsessed with virality. In an era where “radio” means playlists and metrics, Melt is a reminder that music can still feel handmade, intimate, and alive. It’s not a breakup album, not a reinventio,  but a quiet reclamation of voice and space.
